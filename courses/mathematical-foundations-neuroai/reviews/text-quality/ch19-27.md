# Text Quality Review: Chapters 19-27

## Overall Findings for Range

Most of chapters 19-27 are not yet sufficient to teach a beginner. The dominant issue is repeated template text: each section has the same chapter-position paragraph, a generic "typed relationship" formal setup, a repeated anchor equation, a short proof sketch, one worked example, a generic ML/DL/neuroscience connection, and nearly identical exercises. In many sections the inserted section title is the only section-specific element.

The repeated scaffold creates serious section-title mismatches. Examples include Bayes' theorem and a Poisson spike-count example inside "Randomized experiments and neural interventions"; gradient descent inside "Lagrange multipliers", "KKT conditions", and "Duality"; condition-number material inside "Computational graphs" and "Forward-mode and reverse-mode autodiff"; deterministic ODE material inside PDE and stochastic-process chapters; and a smooth-convex theorem inside nonsmooth optimization. These are not small omissions; they would teach beginners the wrong object for the section title.

The one strong exception is Chapter 22 section 4, "Variational calculus." It has a real beginner setup, meaningful notation, an Euler-Lagrange derivation, worked examples, domain connections, and specific exercises. It should be used as the model for rebuilding the rest of this range.

Rating scale: 1 means mostly placeholder or mismatched; 2 means a relevant anchor exists but is too thin or partly mismatched; 3 means partially teachable with important gaps; 4 means mostly teachable with targeted improvements; 5 means sufficient for beginners as written. No section in this range earns a 5.

## Chapter 19 - Causality, Interventions, and Experimental Design

Chapter-level verdict: Insufficient for beginners.

### Section 19.1 - Correlation, causation, and structural models

- Rating: 2/5
- What works: The section correctly flags that \(P(Y \mid \operatorname{do}(X=x))\) generally differs from \(P(Y \mid X=x)\). The pupil-size/performance example is a reasonable beginner entry point for confounding, and the backdoor criterion is at least named.
- What is missing: There is no actual structural causal model definition, no structural equations, no DAG vocabulary beyond vague references, no path-blocking explanation, no adjustment formula, and no beginner explanation of assumptions. The proof sketch is too compressed to be useful.
- Concrete improvement recommendation: Build one complete confounded graph, define variables and structural equations, contrast observation with graph mutilation under intervention, then derive the backdoor adjustment formula step by step on the pupil/task-difficulty example.

### Section 19.2 - Do-calculus intuition

- Rating: 1/5
- What works: It keeps the intervention-vs-observation distinction in view.
- What is missing: The section does not teach do-calculus. It does not state or motivate the three do-calculus rules, explain graph surgery, identify conditional independences in mutilated graphs, or show an identifiable effect that needs more than ordinary backdoor adjustment. The theorem and example are copied from the previous section.
- Concrete improvement recommendation: Replace the repeated backdoor text with an intuitive presentation of the three rules, using small before/after graphs and one worked derivation such as a front-door-style identification example.

### Section 19.3 - Randomized experiments and neural interventions

- Rating: 1/5
- What works: The section mentions neural perturbation elsewhere in the chapter, and randomization is implicitly relevant to intervention language.
- What is missing: The formal setup switches to Bayes' theorem and expectation, and the worked example is a Poisson spike-count calculation. There is no treatment/control setup, assignment mechanism, average treatment effect, randomization argument, blocking, power, controls, optogenetics, stimulation, ablation, or measurement confound discussion.
- Concrete improvement recommendation: Define units, treatment assignment, potential outcomes or interventional distributions, and ATE. Then show why random assignment breaks confounding, followed by a neural stimulation example with controls, outcome measure, and interpretation limits.

### Section 19.4 - Causal representation learning preview

- Rating: 1/5
- What works: It gestures toward robust representation learning and interventions.
- What is missing: It does not explain what a representation is, what makes a representation causal, why latent causal variables are hard to identify, or how interventions, invariances, environments, and out-of-distribution generalization enter. The theorem and example repeat the backdoor material and do not match the title.
- Concrete improvement recommendation: Introduce a simple latent-variable generative process, distinguish predictive features from causal factors, explain identifiability caveats, and give a concrete ML example where intervention-invariant features generalize while spurious features fail.

## Chapter 20 - Optimization Geometry and Convexity

Chapter-level verdict: Insufficient for beginners.

### Section 20.1 - Optimization problems

- Rating: 2/5
- What works: The minimization notation and gradient-descent update are relevant, and the one-dimensional quadratic example is beginner-friendly.
- What is missing: The section does not define an optimization problem carefully: objective, decision variable, feasible set, constraint, local optimum, global optimum, minimizer versus minimum value, and existence assumptions are absent. It jumps to convex optimality before establishing the basic vocabulary.
- Concrete improvement recommendation: Start with \(\min_{\theta \in \Theta} L(\theta)\), define every object, compare unconstrained and constrained examples, and use a simple ML loss plus a neural parameter-fitting example to make the abstraction concrete.

### Section 20.2 - Convex sets and convex functions

- Rating: 2/5
- What works: The stated theorem that zero gradient implies global optimality for smooth convex functions is relevant, and the supporting-hyperplane inequality is the right proof tool.
- What is missing: The section never defines convex sets, convex combinations, convex functions, epigraphs, Jensen's inequality, first-order convexity, or why convexity rules out bad local minima. The core intuition says "Probability," which is a title mismatch.
- Concrete improvement recommendation: Define convexity geometrically with line segments and epigraphs, prove the first-order condition in a small derivation, and include worked examples of a convex quadratic, a nonconvex double well, and logistic-regression loss.

### Section 20.3 - Subgradients and nonsmooth optimization

- Rating: 1/5
- What works: The section at least places nonsmooth optimization inside the optimization chapter.
- What is missing: The anchor equation uses an ordinary gradient, and the theorem assumes smoothness, so the main mathematical content contradicts the section title. There is no definition of subgradient or subdifferential, no subgradient inequality, no kink example, no \(|x|\), hinge loss, ReLU, L1 penalty, or proximal method.
- Concrete improvement recommendation: Use \(f(x)=|x|\) and hinge loss to define the subdifferential. Then show the subgradient inequality, explain why many slopes support the graph at a kink, and add a worked L1/proximal example.

### Section 20.4 - Geometry of regularization

- Rating: 1/5
- What works: The chapter-level optimization framing is relevant to regularization.
- What is missing: The text does not teach regularization geometry. It repeats gradient descent and smooth-convex optimality, with no L1/L2 norm balls, penalty-versus-constraint equivalence, bias-variance intuition, shrinkage, sparsity, early stopping, Bayesian prior view, or ML/neuroscience example.
- Concrete improvement recommendation: Compare ridge and lasso visually and algebraically, showing how Euclidean and diamond-shaped constraint sets produce shrinkage versus sparsity. Connect this to overfitting, MAP priors, and regularized neural decoding.

## Chapter 21 - Gradient Methods, Newton Methods, and Stochastic Optimization

Chapter-level verdict: Insufficient for beginners.

- Rating: 2/5

### Section 21.2 - Momentum and adaptive methods

## Chapter 22 - Constrained Optimization, Duality, and Variational Principles

Chapter-level verdict: Partially sufficient for beginners.

### Section 22.1 - Lagrange multipliers

- Rating: 1/5
- What works: The section correctly sits inside constrained optimization.
- What is missing: It does not define equality constraints, the Lagrangian, multiplier variables, constraint qualification, tangent space, or the geometric condition that objective and constraint gradients align at a constrained optimum. The anchor equation is ordinary gradient descent.
- Concrete improvement recommendation: Use a two-dimensional equality-constrained example, derive \(\nabla f=\lambda \nabla g\) from feasible perturbations, and solve a complete problem such as minimizing distance to a point on a circle.

### Section 22.2 - KKT conditions

- Rating: 1/5

- What is missing: There is no statement of stationarity, primal feasibility, dual feasibility, complementary slackness, inequality constraints, active sets, or constraint qualifications. The repeated theorem is about unconstrained smooth convex optimization, not KKT.

### Section 22.3 - Duality

- Rating: 1/5

- What is missing: The section does not define primal problem, Lagrangian dual function, dual problem, weak duality, strong duality, duality gap, Slater's condition, or why dual variables are shadow prices. The information-theory core intuition is generic and not used.
- Concrete improvement recommendation: Derive the dual function by minimizing the Lagrangian over primal variables, prove weak duality in a few lines, and use a regularized least-squares or SVM-style example to show why the dual viewpoint is useful in ML.

### Section 22.4 - Variational calculus

- What is missing: A beginner could still use more scaffolding around the fundamental lemma, functional derivative notation, second variation or minimum-versus-stationary distinction, and one fully worked ML/neuroscience example rather than only conceptual connections.
- Concrete improvement recommendation: Keep this section as the template. Add a short "what changed from ordinary calculus" table, a more explicit explanation of the fundamental lemma, and one complete variational-inference or neural-field worked example with all objects named.

## Range Priority Fixes

1. Replace template-generated sections with section-specific content. The current scaffold should not be reused without real definitions, equations, derivations, examples, and exercises tied to each title.
2. Fix anchor-equation mismatches first. Bayes' theorem does not belong in numerical conditioning, gradient descent does not teach KKT or duality, deterministic ODEs do not teach PDEs or stochastic processes, and smooth-gradient formulas do not teach nonsmooth optimization.
3. Use Chapter 22 section 4 as the local quality model. Each section should include a beginner-oriented setup, named objects, notation, a meaningful derivation, at least one worked example, specific domain connections, and exercises that cannot be copied to another section unchanged.
4. Add beginner setup before theorems. Define the objects, assumptions, and notation before giving proof sketches. Beginners need to know what problem the theorem solves and when it applies.
5. Replace generic proof sketches with useful derivations. A proof section should either derive the central formula step by step or explain why the theorem is true in a way that prepares the student to use it.
6. Replace generic worked examples with title-specific examples. Every section should have an example that uses the section's own mathematical object, not a nearby topic from the same chapter or volume.
7. Make ML/DL/neuroscience connections concrete. Move from broad lists of applications to one explained connection per section, such as optogenetic intervention design, Adam in deep learning, neural ODEs, Kalman filtering for neural decoding, or neural fields.
8. Rewrite exercises so they are diagnostic and section-specific. The repeated exercise stems should become concrete tasks with the actual equation, theorem, or example from the section.
9. Audit figures and captions after text revision. Several captions describe generic concept plates that match the template rather than the section's actual title; they should be regenerated or relabeled only after the text is corrected.
