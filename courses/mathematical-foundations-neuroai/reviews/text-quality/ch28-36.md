# Text Quality Review: Chapters 28-36

## Overall Findings for Range

Across chapters 28-36, the generated files are not sufficient to teach a beginner the material. The dominant problem is not small omissions; it is that most sections are filled by a repeated template that changes the section name but reuses generic motivating questions, generic "typed relationship" setup prose, generic intuition prompts, a single reused anchor equation, a reused proof sketch, a reused worked example, and the same five exercises.

The template sometimes lands near the topic, as in Chapter 31's risk and empirical-risk equations or Chapter 28's Fourier/convolution anchor. More often it produces section-title mismatches: graph theory sections use Fourier convolution, linear models use continuous-time ODE stability, kernels use Bayes' rule, and several RL sections use supervised ERM. These mismatches would actively confuse beginners because the text implies that the displayed equation is the central object of the titled section.

Mathematical correctness is therefore mixed. Many equations are correct in isolation, but they are often incorrect as teaching anchors for the section in which they appear. Proof/derivation material is almost always a one-sentence sketch, not a usable derivation. Worked examples are usually relevant only to the source template, not to the section title. ML/DL/neuroscience connections are present but broad and list-like, with little mechanism. Exercises are uniformly generic and do not check section-specific understanding.

The range needs a substantive rewrite rather than light editing. Each section should introduce the beginner's problem, define the actual mathematical objects for that title, give one derivation or theorem that belongs to that section, work through a concrete numerical or conceptual example, and end with section-specific exercises.

## Chapter 28 - Signals, Time Series, and Spectral Analysis

Chapter-level verdict: Partially sufficient for beginners.

The chapter has the right general signal-processing anchor, especially Fourier transforms and convolution, but it repeats the same material in all four sections. It does not teach the distinctions among representation, spectra, filters, and neural signals.

### Section 28.1 - Time-series representation

Rating: 2/5.

What works: The section correctly signals that time and frequency views are related, and the Fourier/convolution formulas are relevant to signals in general. The LFP and spike-train example at least names neural time-series modalities.

What is missing: A beginner setup for what a time series is: discrete versus continuous time, sampling interval, units, indexing, observation noise, stationarity, windows, trials, and multichannel recordings. The Fourier transform is introduced before explaining the basic object \(x_t\) or \(x(t)\), so beginners do not know what is being represented. The proof sketch is about convolution, not representation.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Start with a small sampled signal \(x_0,\ldots,x_{T-1}\), define sampling rate and time axis, contrast event trains and continuous traces, then show how the same data can be represented as raw samples, features, autocorrelation, or frequency coefficients. Add an exercise that asks students to label axes and units for an EEG, LFP, and spike-count time series.

### Section 28.2 - Spectral analysis

Rating: 2/5.

What works: The Fourier transform is an appropriate anchor, and the motivating question about time versus frequency is directionally correct. The convolution theorem can be useful later.

What is missing: The section does not define spectrum, power spectral density, amplitude, phase, DFT/FFT, windowing, leakage, sampling, Nyquist frequency, or stationarity. It also does not explain why spectra help with oscillations or rhythmic neural data. The worked example is a filter example rather than a spectral-estimation example.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Replace the generic setup with a worked discrete example: compute the DFT of a short sinusoid-plus-noise signal, interpret peaks, discuss phase and power, and connect the result to EEG bands or LFP oscillations. Add a derivation of the DFT basis interpretation before mentioning the continuous Fourier integral.

### Section 28.3 - Linear filters

Rating: 2/5.

What works: Convolution is a relevant anchor for linear time-invariant filters, and the low-pass LFP example belongs here. The convolution theorem is useful for explaining frequency responses.

What is missing: The section never defines a filter, impulse response, transfer function, linearity, time invariance, causality, FIR/IIR filters, or the difference between smoothing and denoising. It also does not explain how a filter can distort signals or introduce phase delay.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Teach filtering through \(y_t=\sum_k h_k x_{t-k}\), then show a three-point moving average by hand, then connect \(H(\omega)\) to attenuation of high frequencies. Include an example comparing causal and noncausal filters on a neural trace.

### Section 28.4 - Neural signals

Rating: 2/5.

What works: The section names LFP traces and spike trains, and it recognizes that neural measurement pipelines matter. The chapter-level topic is appropriate for neuroscience.

What is missing: It does not distinguish spikes, membrane voltage, LFP, EEG/MEG, calcium imaging, fMRI, or behavior time series. It does not teach preprocessing, binning, filtering choices, spike-rate estimation, trial alignment, artifacts, or interpretation limits. The same convolution theorem and low-pass example are reused without explaining neural-specific consequences.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Build the section around a table of neural signal types with sampling scale, units, noise sources, and common transformations. Add a worked example converting spike times to binned counts and a smoothed rate, then compare that to filtering an LFP trace.

## Chapter 29 - Graphs, Networks, and Spectral Graph Theory

Chapter-level verdict: Insufficient for beginners.

This chapter is dominated by a severe section-title mismatch. Every section uses the signal-processing vocabulary, Fourier/convolution equations, low-pass filtering examples, and time-frequency summary. It does not teach graph theory.

### Section 29.1 - Graph basics

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The section title and chapter placement identify an important prerequisite area. The generic language about objects, variables, and transformations could be repurposed.

What is missing: There are no definitions of vertices, edges, directed/undirected graphs, weighted graphs, adjacency matrices, degree, paths, components, or neighborhoods. The Fourier/convolution equation and low-pass example are unrelated to graph basics.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Replace the anchor with \(G=(V,E)\), adjacency matrix \(A\), and degree \(d_i=\sum_j A_{ij}\). Use a five-node example to compute degrees, paths, connected components, and a simple adjacency matrix.

### Section 29.2 - Graph Laplacians

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The title points to the right central object for spectral graph theory.

What is missing: The text does not define \(L=D-A\), normalized Laplacians, quadratic forms \(x^\top Lx\), smoothness on graphs, nullspaces, connected components, or diffusion. The convolution theorem is not the main derivation for graph Laplacians.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Define \(L=D-A\), derive \(x^\top Lx=\frac12\sum_{ij}A_{ij}(x_i-x_j)^2\), and explain that the Laplacian penalizes neighboring nodes having very different values. Work through a path graph or triangle graph by hand.

### Section 29.3 - Spectral graph methods

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The ML/DL connection line mentions spectral graph methods, so it at least points in the right broad direction.

What is missing: There is no explanation of Laplacian eigenvectors, graph Fourier modes, spectral clustering, graph embeddings, diffusion maps, or graph neural network connections. The figure caption still describes a time signal and filter response, which conflicts with the section title.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Use the Laplacian eigenproblem \(Lu=\lambda u\) as the anchor. Show how the Fiedler vector separates a small graph, then connect this to spectral clustering and graph signal smoothing.

### Section 29.4 - Network science for neuroscience and ML

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The topic is relevant to connectomes, functional connectivity, attention graphs, and GNNs.

What is missing: The text does not mention graph metrics such as centrality, clustering coefficient, modularity, hubs, small-world structure, connectomes, functional versus structural networks, or graph neural networks. The reused low-pass/spike-train example is a mismatch.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Introduce network metrics with one neuroscience example, such as structural connectivity between brain regions, and one ML example, such as message passing on a citation graph. Add exercises computing degree distribution and clustering coefficient on a toy network.

## Chapter 30 - Control Theory and Dynamic Programming

Chapter-level verdict: Insufficient for beginners.

The chapter has a dynamical-systems anchor, but it does not teach control. The equation omits control inputs, costs, policies, Bellman recurrences, and biological feedback examples. Only the first section is near the right domain.

### Section 30.1 - Controlled dynamical systems

Rating: 2/5.

What works: The state-evolution equation and stability example are useful background for dynamical systems. The scalar exponential-decay example is beginner-friendly.

What is missing: The section title says "controlled," but the anchor is uncontrolled: \(\dot x=f(x,t)\) rather than \(\dot x=f(x,u,t)\) or \(\dot x=Ax+Bu\). There is no discussion of inputs, feedback, controllability, observability, state-space diagrams, or control constraints.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Rewrite around \(\dot x=Ax+Bu\) and \(u(t)=Kx(t)\). Use a one-dimensional temperature or reaching-movement example to show how different feedback gains change convergence and stability.

### Section 30.2 - Optimal control

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The text signals that control is related to trajectories through state space.

What is missing: There is no objective functional, running cost, terminal cost, policy, Hamiltonian, Euler-Lagrange/Pontryagin condition, or LQR example. The eigenvalue stability proof does not teach optimal control.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Introduce \(J(u)=\int_0^T c(x(t),u(t))\,dt+\phi(x(T))\) subject to dynamics. Work through a simple tradeoff between reaching a target quickly and using large control effort, then mention LQR as the canonical quadratic-linear case.

### Section 30.3 - Dynamic programming

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The section correctly identifies computation as an intuition category, but only at a generic level.

What is missing: There is no principle of optimality, value function, Bellman equation, finite-horizon recursion, policy extraction, or state-action table. The displayed ODE and eigenvalue proof are not dynamic programming.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Replace the anchor with \(V_t(s)=\min_a[c(s,a)+\mathbb E V_{t+1}(S')]\). Use a tiny gridworld or two-state control problem and compute one Bellman backup by hand.

### Section 30.4 - Control in biological systems

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The chapter at least points to motor control and neural fields in the connection sentence.

What is missing: The section does not explain feedback, delays, noise, internal models, optimal feedback control, homeostasis, reflex arcs, motor planning, or biological constraints. The scalar \(\dot x=-2x\) example is too generic to teach biological control.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Use a biological feedback loop such as postural control, eye movements, reaching, or homeostatic regulation. Show state, sensory feedback, controller, plant, delay/noise, and cost, then connect to optimal feedback control and reinforcement learning.

## Chapter 31 - Learning as Statistical Function Approximation

Chapter-level verdict: Partially sufficient for beginners.

This is the strongest chapter in the range because the displayed risk and empirical-risk equations match the chapter title. However, the four sections are still too repetitive and too thin for beginners.

### Section 31.1 - Formal learning problem

Rating: 3/5.

What works: The risk \(R(f)\) and empirical risk \(\hat R_n(f)\) are appropriate anchors. The connection sentence correctly names function class, loss, data distribution, and optimizer as defining ingredients.

What is missing: A beginner needs definitions of input space, output space, data distribution, training sample, hypothesis class, loss, learner, train/test split, and supervised versus unsupervised settings. The text does not walk through a concrete prediction task.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Start with a small dataset \((x_i,y_i)\), define \(f\in\mathcal F\), \(\ell\), \(P(X,Y)\), \(R(f)\), and \(\hat R_n(f)\), then map each symbol to a simple image-classification or neural-decoding example.

### Section 31.2 - Empirical risk minimization

Rating: 3/5.

What works: The population-risk versus sample-risk contrast is relevant and correct. The section's computational framing fits ERM.

What is missing: It does not define the ERM estimator \(\hat f\in\arg\min_{f\in\mathcal F}\hat R_n(f)\), explain why population risk is inaccessible, or show overfitting. The worked example uses a classifier and kernel method without actually minimizing empirical risk.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Add the ERM optimization problem and a worked least-squares or logistic-regression example where students compute the empirical loss for two candidate models. Show how validation loss can diverge from training loss.

### Section 31.3 - Generalization

Rating: 2/5.

What works: The section names approximation, estimation, and optimization error, which is a useful high-level decomposition.

What is missing: It does not define generalization gap \(R(f)-\hat R_n(f)\), train/test error, iid assumptions, complexity, VC/Rademacher intuition, concentration, or why large neural networks complicate classical bounds. The "proof sketch" is not a bound.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Add a beginner-level derivation of a finite-hypothesis-class union-bound generalization inequality, then interpret each term. Include a worked example showing how sample size and hypothesis-class size affect the bound.

### Section 31.4 - No-free-lunch and inductive bias

Rating: 2/5.

What works: The section title is important, and the chapter-level framing of function classes and losses points toward inductive bias.

What is missing: The section never states a no-free-lunch result, explains what an inductive bias is, or gives examples such as smoothness, locality, equivariance, sparsity, or architectural bias. The geometry intuition line is generic and not tied to the theorem.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Explain that without assumptions about the data distribution, no learner is uniformly best. Then give concrete biases: linear models prefer linear boundaries, CNNs prefer local translation-equivariant structure, and graph neural networks prefer relational message passing.

## Chapter 32 - Linear Models, GLMs, and Classification

Chapter-level verdict: Insufficient for beginners.

The chapter uses a dynamical-systems template throughout. The ODE, stability proof, scalar decay example, and recurrent-network connection do not teach linear models, GLMs, classification, or SVMs.

### Section 32.1 - Linear regression revisited

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The computational intuition could be useful if rewritten around least squares.

What is missing: There is no model \(y=X\beta+\epsilon\), least-squares objective, normal equations, residuals, design matrix, noise assumptions, bias/variance discussion, or relation to maximum likelihood. The displayed ODE is off-topic.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Anchor the section on \(\hat\beta=(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y\) or \(\min_\beta\|X\beta-y\|^2\). Work a two-feature regression example and interpret coefficients, residuals, and prediction error.

### Section 32.2 - Logistic regression and classification

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The section title identifies a core classification method.

What is missing: There is no sigmoid or softmax, log-likelihood, cross-entropy, decision boundary, class label encoding, calibration, or gradient. The scalar exponential-decay example is unrelated.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Define \(p(y=1\mid x)=\sigma(w^\top x+b)\), derive the binary cross-entropy loss from Bernoulli likelihood, and show how the sign of \(w^\top x+b\) gives a linear decision boundary.

### Section 32.3 - Generalized linear models

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The title is a natural bridge between regression and neural GLMs.

What is missing: The text does not explain exponential-family distributions, link functions, linear predictors, mean functions, Poisson/logistic/linear GLM examples, or why neural spike-count models are GLMs. The ODE stability anchor is a section-title mismatch.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Teach GLMs as "linear predictor plus nonlinear link plus noise model." Use \(g(\mathbb E[Y\mid X])=X\beta\), then compare Gaussian identity-link, Bernoulli logit-link, and Poisson log-link models.

### Section 32.4 - Margins and support vector machines

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The title correctly names the core SVM intuition: margins.

What is missing: There is no margin definition, separating hyperplane, hinge loss, support vectors, primal/dual objective, slack variables, or kernel trick. The probability intuition line and ODE proof are incorrect anchors for this material.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Start with a 2D separating line and define geometric margin. Derive the hard-margin SVM objective \(\min_w \frac12\|w\|^2\) subject to \(y_i(w^\top x_i+b)\ge1\), then introduce hinge loss for nonseparable data.

## Chapter 33 - Regularization, Kernels, and Gaussian Processes

Chapter-level verdict: Insufficient for beginners.

The chapter uses probability/Bayes material where only part of the topic calls for it. Regularization as prior knowledge and Gaussian processes can use Bayesian framing, but kernel methods and RKHS need geometry and function-space content that is absent.

### Section 33.1 - Regularization as prior knowledge

Rating: 2/5.

What works: Bayes' rule is loosely relevant to interpreting regularization as a prior. The connection to uncertainty and Bayesian decoding is directionally useful.

What is missing: There is no regularized objective \(L(\theta)+\lambda\Omega(\theta)\), no examples of \(L_2\), \(L_1\), early stopping, dropout, smoothness, or MAP estimation. The Poisson spike-count example teaches likelihood, not regularization.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Show ridge regression as both penalized least squares and MAP with a Gaussian prior. Work through how increasing \(\lambda\) shrinks coefficients and changes bias/variance.

### Section 33.2 - Kernel methods

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The geometry intuition line is in the right broad family.

What is missing: There is no definition of a kernel \(k(x,x')\), feature map \(\phi\), Gram matrix, positive semidefinite condition, kernel trick, or kernelized regression/classification. Bayes' theorem is not the central anchor.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Define \(k(x,x')=\langle\phi(x),\phi(x')\rangle\), compute a small Gram matrix for a polynomial or RBF kernel, and show how kernels allow linear methods in implicit feature spaces.

### Section 33.3 - Reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The section title is appropriate for a mathematical foundations course.

What is missing: There is no Hilbert space setup, reproducing property \(f(x)=\langle f,k(x,\cdot)\rangle_\mathcal H\), norm control, representer theorem, or connection to smoothness. The probability anchor is a mismatch.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Introduce RKHS through the reproducing property and explain why point evaluation becomes an inner product. Then state a beginner-friendly representer theorem and show how it reduces infinite-dimensional optimization to coefficients on training points.

### Section 33.4 - Gaussian processes

Rating: 2/5.

What works: The probability framing and Bayes' rule are relevant background. Gaussian processes are uncertainty models, so the connection sentence is not entirely misplaced.

What is missing: There is no definition of a GP as a distribution over functions, no mean function, covariance kernel, finite-dimensional Gaussian marginals, conditioning formula, posterior mean/variance, or regression example. The Poisson spike-count example is not a GP example.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Define \(f\sim\mathcal{GP}(m,k)\), show that function values at finite inputs are multivariate normal, and work a one-dimensional GP regression example with prior samples, observed points, posterior mean, and posterior uncertainty.

## Chapter 34 - Probabilistic Graphical Models and Latent Variables

Chapter-level verdict: Insufficient for beginners.

This chapter mixes two unrelated templates: ODE stability for most sections and Bayes' rule for EM. It does not teach graphical factorization, latent variables, EM, or approximate inference.

### Section 34.1 - Graphical models

Rating: 1/5.

What is missing: There is no definition of nodes as random variables, edges as dependencies, directed/undirected graphical models, factorization, conditional independence, d-separation, Markov blankets, or factor graphs. The ODE anchor is incorrect.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Use a three-variable DAG \(Z\to X\), \(Z\to Y\) to teach factorization \(p(z,x,y)=p(z)p(x\mid z)p(y\mid z)\). Add a conditional-independence exercise using a small graph.

### Section 34.2 - Latent variable models

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The probability intuition sentence mentions latent variables in passing.

What is missing: The section does not define observed versus hidden variables, marginal likelihood, mixture models, factor analysis, VAEs, or why marginalization is hard. The stability proof and scalar ODE example are unrelated.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Introduce \(p_\theta(x,z)=p_\theta(z)p_\theta(x\mid z)\) and \(p_\theta(x)=\int p_\theta(x,z)\,dz\). Work through a two-component Gaussian mixture as the canonical beginner example.

### Section 34.3 - Expectation-maximization

Rating: 2/5.

What works: Bayes' rule and conditioning are relevant prerequisites, and optimization is an appropriate intuition category for EM.

What is missing: The text does not define latent posteriors, the E-step, M-step, responsibilities, ELBO/free energy, Jensen's inequality, or monotonic likelihood improvement. The Poisson spike-count example does not illustrate EM.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Teach EM on a Gaussian mixture: compute responsibilities in the E-step, update means/weights in the M-step, and show how this alternates between inferring latent assignments and fitting parameters.

### Section 34.4 - Approximate inference

What is missing: There is no posterior \(p(z\mid x)\), intractable marginalization, variational inference, mean-field approximation, message passing, loopy belief propagation, MCMC, amortized inference, or ELBO. The section reverts to ODE stability.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Contrast exact inference by summing/integrating over latent variables with approximate methods. Give one variational example \(q(z)\approx p(z\mid x)\) and one sampling example, with a simple explanation of accuracy versus computation.

## Chapter 36 - Reinforcement Learning

Chapter-level verdict: Insufficient for beginners.

The chapter does not establish the RL setup. Only the policy-optimization section has a broadly relevant optimization anchor, and even there it omits the RL-specific objective and policy-gradient identity.

### Section 36.1 - Markov decision processes

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The title identifies the correct foundation for RL.

What is missing: There is no MDP tuple \((\mathcal S,\mathcal A,P,R,\gamma)\), transition probability, reward, policy, trajectory, return, Markov property, or gridworld example. The ODE stability material is not an MDP setup.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Define the MDP tuple and work through a two-state or gridworld example. Show how action changes transition probabilities and expected rewards.

### Section 36.2 - Value functions and Bellman equations

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The section title is exactly the right next step after MDPs.

What is missing: There is no value function \(V^\pi(s)\), action-value function \(Q^\pi(s,a)\), return, Bellman expectation equation, Bellman optimality equation, temporal-difference error, or value iteration. The ERM formula with labels is a mismatch.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Anchor the section with \(V^\pi(s)=\mathbb E_\pi[R_{t+1}+\gamma V^\pi(S_{t+1})\mid S_t=s]\). Compute one Bellman backup in a small MDP and explain value as "expected future reward from here."

### Section 36.3 - Policy optimization

Rating: 2/5.

What works: Optimization is relevant, and a gradient update is a useful general template. The worked example of gradient descent is beginner-friendly.

What is missing: The objective is a generic supervised-learning loss, not expected return \(J(\theta)\). The convex optimality theorem can mislead because policy optimization is usually nonconvex and stochastic. There is no policy gradient theorem, score-function estimator, actor-critic idea, exploration, or variance/baseline discussion.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Replace the generic loss with \(J(\theta)=\mathbb E_{\tau\sim\pi_\theta}[\sum_t\gamma^t r_t]\) and derive the basic score-function policy gradient at an intuitive level. Use a two-action bandit to show how increasing log-probability of rewarded actions changes the policy.

### Section 36.4 - RL and neuroscience

Rating: 1/5.

What works: The topic is important for dopamine, reward prediction error, habit learning, and decision-making.

What is missing: The section does not discuss reward prediction error, temporal-difference learning, dopamine, basal ganglia, exploration/exploitation, model-based versus model-free control, successor representations, or links to behavior. The ERM/scaling-law text is unrelated.

Concrete improvement recommendation: Build around temporal-difference learning: \(\delta_t=r_t+\gamma V(s_{t+1})-V(s_t)\). Explain how this resembles reward prediction error signals, then compare RL algorithms with biological learning cautiously.

## Range Priority Fixes

1. Replace reused template anchors with section-specific mathematical anchors. Graph sections need graph objects, control sections need inputs/costs/Bellman equations, linear-model sections need regression/classification objectives, kernel sections need kernels/RKHS/GP definitions, and RL sections need MDP/value/policy-gradient equations.

2. Add beginner setup before formulas. Each section should define the object, notation, assumptions, units or dimensions, and the first concrete example before presenting the main equation.

3. Remove section-title mismatches. Audit every motivating question, formal vocabulary list, proof sketch, worked example, figure caption, chapter summary entry, and exercise prompt against the section title.

4. Turn proof sketches into teachable derivations. One short but complete derivation per section is better than a generic one-sentence sketch. State assumptions explicitly and explain what the result is used for.

5. Replace generic worked examples with section-specific examples. Use tiny numerical examples: DFT of a short signal, Laplacian of a small graph, Bellman backup in a gridworld, logistic regression on two points, \(k\)-means assignment/update, GP posterior on one-dimensional data.

6. Make ML/DL/neuroscience connections mechanistic. Instead of lists, explain how the mathematical object appears in a model or neural-data workflow and what can go wrong when misapplied.

7. Rewrite exercises to test the section. Keep a mix of mechanical, derivation, implementation, interpretation, and misconception prompts, but make each one use the actual equations, examples, and concepts from that section.

8. Prioritize complete rewrites for Chapters 29, 32, 34, 35, and 36. Chapters 28, 30, 31, and 33 have some reusable anchors, but still need section-specific expansion before they can support beginner learning.
